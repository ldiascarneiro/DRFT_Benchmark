### **Abstract**  
This paper introduces the **interactive bAbI dataset**, an extension of the original bAbI tasks that incorporates **supporting questions, synthetic feedback, and user interaction** to better model real-world question-answering scenarios. We propose a **two-stage GRU-based architecture** that distinguishes between original context and feedback, enabling the model to dynamically adjust its reasoning process. Our approach achieves **state-of-the-art performance** on the extended dataset compared to baselines like DMN and MemN2N, while addressing limitations such as synthetic question generation and limited task coverage. However, the model is currently evaluated on only **three of the 20 bAbI tasks**, and further work is needed to validate its scalability and generalization to real-world feedback loops.  

---

### **Introduction**  
Existing question-answering datasets, such as the original bAbI tasks, lack **feedback mechanisms** that are critical for modeling human-like interaction. While prior work (e.g., DMN) has explored context-aware reasoning, these models **fail to distinguish between original context and user feedback**, leading to suboptimal performance in dynamic scenarios. To address this, we introduce the **interactive bAbI dataset**, which includes **supporting questions, synthetic user feedback, and multi-turn interactions**. Our model, a **two-stage GRU architecture**, explicitly separates feedback processing from original context, enabling it to adapt reasoning strategies based on user input. This work advances the field by providing a **novel dataset and architecture** for interactive QA, while highlighting the need for future research on real-world feedback integration.  

---

### **Related Work**  
Recent advances in question-answering have focused on **contextual reasoning** (e.g., DMN, MemN2N) and **dialogue systems** (e.g., Dialog-based Language Learning by Weston et al. [1], Learning End-to-End Goal-Oriented Dialog by Bordes et al. [2]). However, these models **do not explicitly model feedback loops** or synthetic question generation. Our work differs by **incorporating user feedback as an input modality** and using **RNN-based synthetic question generation** to simulate interactive scenarios. This approach aligns with the goals of **goal-oriented dialogue systems** but extends them to the domain of reasoning tasks.

### **Interactive bAbI Dataset**  
#### **Dataset Extension**  
The interactive bAbI dataset extends the original 20 tasks by adding **supporting questions, synthetic user feedback, and multi-turn interactions**. Supporting questions are generated using **predefined templates** (e.g., "Is the [object] inside the [container]?") and **randomly sampled words** from the original context. User feedback is synthesized using **rule-based heuristics** (e.g., "The [object] is to the left of the [container]") to mimic human-like corrections.  

#### **Necessity of the Interactive Version**  
Existing datasets lack **dynamic feedback mechanisms** that are essential for modeling real-world interactions. The interactive version addresses this gap by enabling models to **adapt reasoning based on user input**, a capability not explored in prior work.

### **Model Architecture**  
Our model employs a **two-stage GRU encoding** to process original context and feedback separately. The first GRU encodes the **original context** (with hidden size $ K_h $), while the second GRU processes **feedback** (with hidden size $ K_f $). To ensure consistency, $ K_f $ is **equal to $ K_h $** (Section 4.5).  

#### **Feedback Integration**  
Feedback is integrated via **attention mechanisms** that weigh the relevance of feedback sentences relative to the original context. This allows the model to **suppress unrelated feedback** in long stories (see Table 6).  

#### **Synthetic Question Generation**  
A **RNN decoder** generates synthetic questions, enabling dynamic interaction. This approach is preferred over direct prediction due to its **flexibility in handling open-ended questions**.

The model is trained using a **multi-task loss function** that combines:

### **Introduction**  
Reinforcement learning (RL) has traditionally relied on explicit reward signals and environmental feedback. However, model-based RL requires accurate predictions of both state transitions and reward outcomes, a challenge that prior work has not fully addressed. While [Oh et al. 2015] introduced a frame prediction model that implicitly captures reward dynamics through score counters, our work explicitly models the joint distribution of frames and rewards, enabling a unified framework for model-based RL. This contribution is foundational, as it provides a pre-condition for integrating learned dynamics into model-based RL pipelines.  

Despite the deterministic nature of Atari games, which may limit the perceived novelty of our approach, the framework’s architecture and training methodology are generalizable to non-deterministic environments. We argue that this work represents a critical stepping stone for future research in model-based RL, even if the results are incremental.

### **Related Work**  
Prior work on frame prediction, such as [Oh et al. 2015], focuses on reconstructing visual observations using recurrent neural networks (RNNs). Recent advances in autoencoders [Finn et al. 2016] have demonstrated the feasibility of learning latent representations for visuomotor tasks. Our model combines frame and reward prediction into a single architecture, a non-trivial task due to the need for alignment between latent representations and training stability. Compared to [Oh et al. 2015], which implicitly captures reward dynamics through score counters, our model explicitly predicts rewards, enabling more direct integration with model-based RL algorithms.
