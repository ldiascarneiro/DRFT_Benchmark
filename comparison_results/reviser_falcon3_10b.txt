**Abstract**

We present a method for learning a joint predictive model of frames and rewards in Atari games. By leveraging this model, we aim to advance model-based reinforcement learning (RL), enabling agents to make better decisions with fewer interactions with the environment. Our approach combines frame prediction, which captures the dynamics of the game, with reward prediction, which informs the agent about the value of its actions. This joint model can significantly enhance the efficiency and effectiveness of RL algorithms, particularly in complex environments where direct interaction is costly or impractical.

**Introduction**

Reinforcement learning (RL) has made significant strides in recent years, thanks to advances in deep learning. However, a key challenge remains: how to learn effective policies with minimal interaction with the environment. Model-based RL offers a promising solution by leveraging learned models of the environment to plan and optimize actions. In this context, predicting both the visual states (frames) and the rewards is crucial. Frames capture the dynamics and state transitions, while rewards provide the immediate feedback on the quality of actions. By learning a joint model of these two aspects, we can better simulate the environment and improve the agent's decision-making process. This paper explores the development and evaluation of such a joint predictive model in the context of Atari games, a popular benchmark for RL research.

**Related Work**

Our work builds on the extensive literature in model-based RL and learned models. Recent advancements have focused on learning models that can predict both frames and rewards to simulate environments more accurately. For instance, [Bellemare et al., 2013] introduced the Arcade Learning Environment (ALE), which has been pivotal for evaluating RL agents. [Bellemare et al., 2016] proposed using count-based exploration to improve learning efficiency. [Dosovitskiy et al., 2015] demonstrated the power of convolutional neural networks in generating complex visual structures. [Finn et al., 2016] explored deep spatial autoencoders for visuomotor learning. Our approach integrates these insights, specifically focusing on the joint prediction of frames and rewards to enhance model-based RL.

**Model**

Our predictive model is designed to capture both the visual dynamics (frames) and the immediate feedback (rewards) in Atari games. The architecture consists of a deep convolutional neural network (CNN) that processes the input frames and predicts the next frame and reward. This dual prediction capability allows the model to simulate the environment effectively, enabling agents to plan their actions based on predicted outcomes. The use of a CNN is well-suited for this task due to its ability to capture spatial hierarchies in visual data, which is crucial for understanding game states.

**Experiments**

We conducted experiments to evaluate the performance of our joint predictive model in predicting both frames and rewards. Our results show that the model can accurately predict future states and rewards over multiple steps, outperforming baseline models that predict either frames or rewards independently. We also demonstrate the practical benefits of this joint model by integrating it into an RL agent, showing improved learning efficiency and performance on several Atari games. These experiments highlight the potential of our approach in advancing model-based RL by providing a more comprehensive and accurate simulation of the environment.

**Discussion**

While our work demonstrates the feasibility and benefits of learning a joint predictive model of frames and rewards, there are limitations and areas for future exploration. One limitation is the incremental nature of our results, which primarily focus on Atari games. Future work could extend this approach to more complex and diverse environments, such as real-world robotics tasks. Additionally, exploring how to incorporate uncertainty in predictions could further enhance the robustness of the learned models. Despite these challenges, our findings suggest a promising direction for advancing model-based RL by leveraging joint predictive models.

**Conclusion**

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel approach to learning a joint predictive model of frames and rewards in Atari games. By effectively predicting both the visual states and the immediate rewards, our model provides a powerful tool for simulating environments and improving the efficiency of reinforcement learning algorithms. This work contributes to the broader goal of developing more intelligent and autonomous agents that can learn and adapt with minimal direct interaction with the environment. Future research will focus on extending these models to more complex scenarios and integrating them with advanced planning algorithms to further enhance their capabilities.

## Introduction

Reinforcement learning (RL) has made significant strides, particularly with deep RL methods that combine neural networks with RL algorithms. However, purely model-free RL approaches have limitations, such as slow learning and poor sample efficiency. Model-based RL, which involves learning a model of the environment, can mitigate these issues by providing a structured way to plan and learn. Our work focuses on integrating frame prediction with reward prediction, a crucial step towards building more comprehensive models that can be used in conjunction with RL algorithms.

## Related Work

Model-based RL has been a topic of interest for decades, with recent advancements leveraging deep learning techniques. The Arcade Learning Environment (ALE) has been pivotal in evaluating RL agents on Atari games. Our work builds upon the success of deep frame predictors, such as those using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) units, which have shown remarkable performance in predicting game frames. We extend this by incorporating reward prediction, a novel contribution that aligns with the broader goal of creating more robust and versatile models for RL.

## Model

Our model is an extension of a state-of-the-art frame predictor. It uses a deep neural network architecture that includes CNNs for processing visual inputs and LSTMs for capturing temporal dependencies. We introduce an additional output layer to predict rewards, alongside the predicted frames. This dual prediction capability allows our model to learn a more comprehensive representation of the environment, which can be beneficial for planning and decision-making in RL tasks.

## Experiments

We evaluated our model on five Atari games, comparing its performance against a baseline that only predicts frames. The results demonstrate that our model not only predicts frames accurately but also successfully predicts rewards, maintaining a unimodal and zero-centered distribution of cumulative reward errors. This indicates that the model captures the essential dynamics of the games, paving the way for future integration with RL algorithms.

## Discussion

While our model shows promising results, it is important to acknowledge its limitations. The deterministic nature of Atari games may lead to overfitting, and the model's performance could vary across different environments. Future work will focus on addressing these challenges, particularly by experimenting with artificial samples and integrating the model with Monte-Carlo tree search for planning.

## Conclusion

This work represents a significant step towards integrating learned models with RL algorithms. By successfully predicting both frames and rewards, we have laid the groundwork for more advanced applications, such as generating artificial samples and using Monte-Carlo tree search for planning. Future research will explore these directions, aiming to enhance the robustness and versatility of our model in real-world scenarios.

**Revised Abstract**

This approach allows for the learning of both dynamics and rewards, which is crucial for model-based reinforcement learning (RL) in real-world environments. Our model builds on recent advances in frame prediction and extends them by incorporating a reward-predicting head. **Revised Introduction**

Reinforcement learning (RL) has made significant strides in recent years, particularly in the domain of Atari games. However, a key challenge remains: the reliance on model-free methods, which can be inefficient and data-intensive. Model-based RL, on the other hand, offers a promising alternative by learning a model of the environment's dynamics and rewards. This approach is particularly valuable for real-world applications where data collection is costly or impractical. In this paper, we focus on a critical aspect of model-based RL: the ability to learn both the transition dynamics and the reward function from raw sensory inputs. We present a deep learning model that achieves this by simultaneously predicting frames and rewards in Atari games, a setting that provides a rich testbed for evaluating such methods.

**Revised Related Work**

Our work is closely related to recent advancements in frame prediction and reward prediction. Frame prediction has seen significant progress with the development of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs), particularly in the context of Atari games (Bellemare et al., 2013; Dosovitskiy et al., 2015). These models have demonstrated the ability to generate realistic game frames, paving the way for more sophisticated applications. On the other hand, reward prediction has been less explored, with early works focusing on supervised learning approaches (Bellemare et al., 2013). Our model builds on these foundations by integrating both frame and reward prediction into a single architecture, leveraging the strengths of deep learning to learn complex representations from raw pixel inputs. This approach not only advances the state of the art in Atari games but also provides a framework for applying model-based RL to more challenging real-world scenarios.

**Revised Model**

Our model is a deep neural network designed to predict both frames and rewards from raw pixel inputs. The architecture consists of a convolutional encoder followed by a recurrent decoder, which has been shown to be effective for frame prediction (Gregor et al., 2015). To incorporate reward prediction, we add a separate head to the network that takes the encoded state as input and predicts the immediate reward. This head is trained using a mean squared error loss, encouraging the model to accurately predict rewards. The combined loss function ensures that the model learns to generate both realistic frames and accurate reward predictions, which are essential for effective model-based RL.

**Revised Experiments**

We conducted experiments on five Atari games to evaluate the performance of our model. The dataset was collected by running an agent in the game environment, recording pixel frames and corresponding rewards. The data was preprocessed to standardize inputs and normalize rewards. The model was trained using a combination of frame prediction loss and reward prediction loss, with hyperparameters optimized through cross-validation. Evaluation metrics included the mean squared error for reward prediction and visual inspection of generated frames. Results showed successful prediction over multiple steps, with zero-centered and unimodal error distributions indicating accurate reward predictions.

**Revised Discussion**

Our findings demonstrate the feasibility of learning both frame and reward predictions from raw pixel inputs in Atari games. While these results are not surprising given the success of deep learning models in similar tasks, they highlight the potential of our approach for more complex applications. The ability to generate accurate reward predictions alongside realistic frames is a significant step towards developing robust model-based RL systems. However, our approach has limitations, particularly in handling the high-dimensional and stochastic nature of real-world environments. Future work will address these challenges by augmenting training with artificial samples and incorporating advanced planning techniques like Monte-Carlo tree search.

**Revised Conclusion**

In this paper, we presented a deep learning model that simultaneously predicts frames and rewards in Atari games. This approach represents a significant step towards model-based RL, where both dynamics and rewards are learned from raw sensory inputs. Our experiments demonstrated the effectiveness of our model, showing successful prediction over multiple steps in five different games. Future work will focus on extending this framework to more challenging environments and incorporating advanced planning methods. By combining frame and reward prediction, we provide a foundation for developing robust and efficient RL systems that can be applied to a wide range of real-world problems.
