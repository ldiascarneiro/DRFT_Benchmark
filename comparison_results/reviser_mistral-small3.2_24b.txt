**Title: Interactive Question Answering with Two-Stage Encoding and User Feedback**

**Abstract**
We propose a novel model for interactive question answering (QA) that extends existing QA datasets with supporting questions and user feedback. Our approach employs a two-stage encoding mechanism to capture both word-level and sentence-level semantics, enabling the model to handle complex reasoning tasks. We introduce an interactive QA dataset derived from the bAbI tasks, where supporting questions and feedback are generated using predefined templates. Experiments show that our model outperforms baselines like DMN and MemN2N, demonstrating the effectiveness of the two-stage encoding and feedback integration.

**1. Introduction**
Existing QA models struggle with interactive tasks where user feedback is required to refine answers. While models like DMN and MemN2N excel in static QA, they lack mechanisms to incorporate dynamic feedback. Our model addresses this gap by introducing a two-stage encoding mechanism that captures hierarchical semantic dependencies. We extend the bAbI dataset with supporting questions and feedback, creating a benchmark for interactive QA. Our contributions include:
- A two-stage encoding mechanism for interactive QA.
- An interactive QA dataset with supporting questions and feedback.
- Empirical results demonstrating superior performance over baselines.

Prior work in QA has focused on static datasets, with models like DMN and MemN2N achieving strong results. However, these models do not handle interactive settings where user feedback is required. Our work builds on these foundations by incorporating feedback into a two-stage encoding framework.

**Title: Joint Frame and Reward Prediction for Model-Based Reinforcement Learning**

**Abstract**
We present a method for jointly predicting frames and rewards in reinforcement learning environments. By extending prior work on frame prediction, we introduce a reward-predicting head that enables the model to anticipate future rewards. Experiments on Atari games demonstrate successful cumulative reward prediction over at least 20 steps (80 frames), establishing a precondition for model-based reinforcement learning.

**1. Introduction**
Model-based reinforcement learning (RL) holds promise for efficient decision-making, but its success depends on accurate predictions of future states and rewards. While prior work has focused on frame prediction (e.g., [Oh et al. 2015]), reward prediction remains underexplored. This paper bridges this gap by introducing a method for jointly predicting frames and rewards, enabling the model to anticipate future outcomes. Our approach establishes a foundation for model-based RL, where accurate reward predictions are critical for planning.

**2. Related Work**
Prior work on frame prediction (e.g., [Oh et al. 2015]) has demonstrated success in predicting future states, but these methods do not account for reward prediction. Separately, reward prediction has been studied in the context of intrinsic motivation (e.g., [Bellemare et al. 2016]), but these methods do not integrate with frame prediction. Our work unifies these two lines of research, enabling joint prediction of frames and rewards. This integration is crucial for model-based RL, where both state and reward predictions are necessary for planning.

**3. Methodology**
Our method extends the frame prediction architecture of [Oh et al. 2015] by adding a reward-predicting head. The model consists of a feedforward network that processes input frames and predicts future frames and rewards. The reward-predicting head is trained to minimize the mean squared error between predicted and ground-truth rewards. This design choice allows the model to learn a compact representation of the environment that captures both visual and reward dynamics.

**4. Experiments**
We evaluate our method on five Atari games: Breakout, Pong, Space Invaders, Seaquest, and Beam Rider. The model successfully predicts cumulative rewards over at least 20 steps (80 frames), as evidenced by zero-centered and unimodal error distributions (Figure 2). However, performance degrades beyond 50 steps due to the stochastic nature of Atari games. Figure 3 illustrates failure cases, such as the model's inability to anticipate objects entering from the right in Seaquest.

**5. Results**
The experimental results demonstrate the feasibility of joint frame and reward prediction. The model achieves accurate reward predictions over short horizons, establishing a precondition for model-based RL. However, the deterministic assumptions of the model limit its performance in highly stochastic environments. Future work could explore probabilistic extensions to address this limitation.

**6. Future Work**
This work establishes a foundation for model-based RL by enabling joint frame and reward prediction. Future directions include:
- **Model-Based RL**: Using the model for planning with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) or other model-based algorithms.
- **Complex Environments**: Testing the method in continuous control tasks or more realistic simulations.
- **Probabilistic Extensions**: Incorporating uncertainty to improve performance in stochastic environments.

**7. Conclusion**
We have presented a method for jointly predicting frames and rewards in reinforcement learning environments. Experiments on Atari games demonstrate successful reward prediction over short horizons, establishing a precondition for model-based RL. While challenges remain, this work represents a step toward more general model-based approaches.
