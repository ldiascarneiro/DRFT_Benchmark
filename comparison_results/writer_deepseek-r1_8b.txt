**3. Proposed Approach**

**3.1 Context-Aware Network (CAN)**

The CAN model consists of three main components: (1) Hierarchical Encoding, (2) Feedback Attention, and (3) Context Fusion.

*   **Hierarchical Encoding:** CAN first encodes the initial question-answer pair using a bidirectional GRU (Bi-GRU). The question `Q = (q1, q2, ..., qL)` and answer `A = (a1, a2, ..., aM)` are encoded separately. The question encoding produces a context vector `C_q ∈ R^K_h`, where `K_h` is the hidden size. The answer encoding produces a context vector `C_a ∈ R^K_h`.

*   **Feedback Attention:** Given a feedback sentence `F = (f1, f2, ..., fN)`, CAN computes attention weights `α = softmax([α1, α2, ..., αN])` over the feedback sentence using a feed-forward network `W_F`. These weights determine the importance of each word in the feedback sentence.

*   **Context Fusion:** The feedback-aware context is computed as `C_f = ∑_i α_i * h_f_i`, where `h_f_i` is the hidden state of the feedback sentence at position `i`. This vector is then combined with the initial context `C_q` using a gated mechanism: `C_combined = g(C_q, C_f)`, where `g` is a learnable gating function.

**3.2 Training Objective**

CAN is trained using a cross-entropy loss function. Given the input question `Q`, the model predicts the final answer `Y` based on the combined context `C_combined`. The loss is computed as `L = -∑_y log(P(y))`, where `P(y)` is the probability of the correct answer `y`.

**Authors:** [Your Name(s)]

**1. Introduction**

Reinforcement Learning (RL) agents learn to take actions in an environment to maximize a cumulative reward signal. Model-based RL approaches aim to learn an internal model of the environment dynamics and rewards, which can then be used for planning. Accurate models are crucial for effective planning, especially in complex environments. Recent advances in deep learning have enabled powerful models for predicting future states or observations (often images or video frames) from past observations and actions [1, 2]. These visual predictors can serve as a basis for learning the underlying dynamics model.

However, the reward signal in RL is equally important, often defining the goal of the agent. While the reward function might be explicitly provided (like a score counter in Atari games), in many real-world scenarios, the reward is sparse, complex, or even unknown. The ability to predict the reward signal is therefore a critical component for successful model-based RL. While frame prediction models implicitly encode information about the environment's state, directly predicting the reward as a separate output might be more efficient and interpretable, especially for complex or partially observable reward structures.

This paper introduces a novel neural network architecture that jointly learns to predict future video frames and the associated reward signal. Our key contribution is the integration of a dedicated reward prediction head within the frame prediction network architecture. We train this joint model end-to-end on sequences of observations and actions from Atari games, optimizing a combined objective for both frame prediction and reward prediction. While previous work, such as Oh et al. [2], has successfully predicted known reward structures in Atari by decoding state, our approach explicitly models the reward as a distinct component, potentially improving accuracy and providing a clearer pathway for predicting more complex or unknown reward functions.

We validate our model on several Atari environments. Our results show that our joint model can accurately predict both future video frames and the reward signal, often achieving better performance than a baseline model that only predicts frames. This demonstrates the feasibility of our approach in a challenging domain. Furthermore, we explore the potential application of our learned model by using it to generate synthetic training data for RL agents, which could potentially improve data efficiency.

While the specific task (predicting rewards in deterministic Atari games) builds upon existing work, the core contribution lies in the novel architectural design that explicitly integrates reward prediction within the frame prediction framework and the demonstration of its effectiveness. This work establishes a foundation for future research on predicting richer reward structures and integrating these predictions into more advanced model-based RL techniques, such as MCTS agents that leverage accurate state and reward forecasts.

**2. Related Work**

Deep learning has revolutionized visual prediction. Oh et al. [2] pioneered the use of convolutional LSTMs for predicting future frames in Atari games, demonstrating state-of-the-art results. Their model implicitly encodes the environment state and dynamics, allowing it to predict future frames. While their model implicitly captures the reward signal (as the agent receives the reward for the actions taken to reach those frames), the reward prediction itself is not an explicit output of the model; it is derived from the state or the frame content.

Our work builds upon this foundation but introduces a key distinction: we explicitly incorporate a reward prediction head. This allows us to directly optimize the prediction of the reward signal, potentially decoupling it from the frame prediction task, even though the underlying state representation is shared. This modular approach might be more effective for certain types of reward functions, especially those that are not directly tied to easily observable state changes or require combining multiple frame features in a specific way.

Other works focus on predicting dynamics or state transitions [1, 3]. While related, our focus is specifically on the joint prediction of observations (frames) and rewards. Some approaches aim to learn a latent state representation from observations, which can then be used for prediction [4]. Our model implicitly learns a latent representation via the frame predictor, but adds the explicit task of predicting the reward from this representation or the input frames.

The prediction of reward signals is also relevant in inverse RL or learning reward functions from demonstrations [5]. However, our setting is different: we aim to predict the reward *within the context of the environment dynamics*, given the current observation and action, much like predicting the next frame. This is a crucial step towards enabling model-based agents to anticipate the consequences of their actions, including the associated reward.

Our work is also conceptually related to models that predict multiple aspects of the future, such as predicting actions or dynamics alongside observations [6]. Our approach extends this by adding the reward signal as an explicit output.

In summary, while our work leverages and builds upon successful frame prediction techniques in Atari, the novelty lies in the architectural choice to explicitly model and predict the reward signal as a separate component within the same neural network framework. **3. Method**

We present our joint video and reward prediction model. The architecture is based on a convolutional LSTM network, similar to the state-of-the-art frame predictor of Oh et al. [2], but augmented with a dedicated reward prediction head.

**3.1. Architecture**

Our model takes as input a sequence of `t` consecutive observations (frames) `O = {o_1, o_2, ..., o_t}` and a sequence of actions `A = {a_1, a_2, ..., a_{t-1}}` (the action taken to transition from `o_i` to `o_{i+1}`). The goal is to predict the subsequent sequence of observations `O' = {o_{t+1}, o_{t+2}, ..., o_{t+T}}` and the reward sequence `R = {r_{t+1}, r_{t+2}, ..., r_{t+T}}`, where `T` is the prediction horizon.

The core of our model is a shared encoder-decoder architecture (Figure 1). The encoder processes the input frames `o_1` to `o_t` and the action sequence `A`. It consists of `N` stacked convolutional LSTM cells, each followed by a convolutional upsampling layer (deconvolution). The input to the encoder at each time step `i` is the concatenated frame `o_i` and the previous action `a_{i-1}` (with `a_0` being a dummy action or action embedding for the first input frame).

Specifically, the input to the `i`-th time step of the encoder is:
`x_i = [o_i, a_{i-1}]` (concatenation)

Each element `x_i` is processed by the stacked LSTM layers. The output of the top LSTM layer is then passed through a deconvolution layer to produce a feature map `h_i`. This feature map `h_i` is then used by the decoder to predict the future frames and rewards.

The decoder is responsible for predicting the future sequence `O'` and `R`. It is a separate set of layers, potentially with different architectures, designed to take the encoded features `h_i` (or `h_{t-i}` depending on the decoder design) and generate the desired outputs.

**3.2. Encoder**

The encoder processes the input sequence `O` and `A`. At each time step `i` (from 1 to `t`), the input `x_i` is a concatenation of the current frame `o_i` and the previous action `a_{i-1}`. This input is fed into a stack of `N` convolutional LSTM cells [7]. Each LSTM cell processes the input and updates its hidden state and cell state. The output of the top LSTM layer is a feature map `h_i` of a fixed size (e.g., 7x7 feature maps). This feature map `h_i` is then passed through a deconvolution layer to produce a feature map `s_i` of a larger size (e.g., 21x21), matching the spatial dimensions of the input frames. This `s_i` is the encoded representation for time step `i`.

**3.3. Decoder**

The decoder takes the encoded features from the encoder and predicts the future sequence `O'` and `R`. There are two main approaches for the decoder: using the encoded features sequentially or using the final encoded feature for the entire prediction horizon. We adopt a sequential approach for better accuracy.

For predicting the `k`-th future frame `o_{t+k}` (k=1 to `T`), the decoder uses the encoded feature `s_{t+k-1}` (the feature from the encoder corresponding to the time step just before the prediction). This feature `s_{t+k-1}` is fed into a separate decoder LSTM cell (or a set of cells). The output of this decoder LSTM is then passed through a deconvolution layer to produce the predicted frame `o'_{t+k}`.

Similarly, for predicting the `k`-th future reward `r_{t+k}`, we use the same `s_{t+k-1}` feature. This feature is fed into a dedicated reward decoder LSTM cell (or a separate network). The output of this reward decoder is a scalar value representing the predicted reward `r_{t+k}`. The architecture of this reward decoder can be simple (e.g., a linear layer) or more complex, depending on the nature of the reward.

**3.4. Objective Function**

Our model is trained to minimize two separate losses: the frame prediction loss and the reward prediction loss, combined into a single objective function.

*   **Frame Prediction Loss:** For each future frame `o'_{t+k}`, we compute the pixel-wise Mean Squared Error (MSE) between the predicted frame `o'_{t+k}` and the true next frame `o_{t+k}`. We also consider the temporal consistency of the predicted frames. The total frame loss `L_frame` is the average MSE over all predicted frames and time steps.
    `L_frame = (1/(T * H * W * N)) * sum_{k=1}^{T} sum_{i=1}^{N} MSE(o'_{t+k}, o_{t+k})`
    where `H` and `W` are the height and width of the frames, and `N` is the number of frames used for prediction (or a weighting factor).

*   **Reward Prediction Loss:** For each future reward `r_{t+k}`, we compute the absolute error or squared error between the predicted reward `r'_k` and the true reward `r_{t+k}`. The total reward loss `L_reward` is the average error over all predicted rewards and time steps.
    `L_reward = (1/T) * sum_{k=1}^{T} |r'_k - r_{t+k}|` (using absolute error for robustness, or MSE)

The total training loss `L_total` is a combination of these two losses:
`L_total = alpha * L_frame + beta * L_reward`
where `alpha` and `beta` are hyperparameters balancing the importance of frame prediction versus reward prediction.

**3.5. Training**

We train our model end-to-end using the `L_total` loss. We use a dataset of consecutive frame-action sequences from the Atari games. For each training step, we select a random starting time step `t` and action sequence `A` (from `t` to `t+T-1`). The input to the model is `O` (frames `o_1` to `o_t`) and `A` (actions `a_1` to `a_{t-1}`), and the target is `O'` (frames `o_{t+1}` to `o_{t+T}`) and `R` (rewards `r_{t+1}` to ` (t+T)`). We use stochastic gradient descent (SGD) or an optimizer like Adam to minimize `L_total`.

**4. Experiments**

We evaluate our model on a set of standard Atari games (e.g., Breakout, Pong, Seaquest) using the Arcade Learning Environment (ALE) [8]. We use the preprocessed frames (84x84 RGB) and actions as described in the relevant papers [2, 9].

**4.1. Baselines**

We compare our model against:
1.  **Frame-Only Predictor (Baseline):** The standard frame prediction model of Oh et al. [2] (using a single convolutional LSTM). This model does not predict rewards.
2.  **No Model:** A policy gradient method (REINFORCE) as a baseline for comparison without model-based prediction.

**4.2. Evaluation Metrics**

We use two main metrics:
1.  **Frame Prediction Accuracy:** Measured using the Mean Squared Error (MSE) between predicted and true frames for a fixed prediction horizon (e.g., 5 frames).
2.  **Reward Prediction Accuracy:** Measured using the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) between predicted and true rewards for the same prediction horizon.

Additionally, we evaluate the potential of our model for generating synthetic training data for RL agents. We use the predicted frames and rewards to simulate trajectories for an agent trained using SARSA(0) on a subset of tasks. We measure the performance (average reward) of this agent trained on a combination of real and synthetic data versus real data alone.

**4.3. Implementation Details**

*   **Network Architecture:** We use a similar architecture to [2], with 3 stacked convolutional LSTMs (each with 32 filters of 5x5) and a deconvolution layer for frame prediction. The reward prediction head uses a separate LSTM layer followed by a linear layer for the scalar output.
*   **Prediction Horizon:** We set `T = 5` frames for most evaluations.
*   **Loss Balancing:** We found `alpha = 0.9` and `beta = 0.1` to work reasonably well, prioritizing frame accuracy slightly.
*   **Training:** We use Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.0001. We train for 50 epochs.

**5. Results**

**5.1. Frame and Reward Prediction Accuracy**

Our results show that the joint model achieves competitive frame prediction accuracy, comparable to the frame-only baseline (Table 1). More importantly, it significantly improves reward prediction accuracy (Table 2). The MAE for reward prediction is consistently lower for our model than for the frame-only baseline, demonstrating the effectiveness of explicitly modeling the reward.

**5.2. Synthetic Data Generation**

We use our model to generate synthetic trajectories (sequences of frames and rewards) for training an RL agent. We find that training an agent on a mix of real data and data generated by our model (with some noise added to the predictions) leads to slightly higher average rewards on the test tasks compared to training solely on real data (Figure 2). This suggests that our model can be a valuable tool for data-efficient RL.

**6. Discussion**

Our experiments confirm that the proposed joint video and reward prediction model is effective. By explicitly incorporating a reward prediction head, we achieve significantly better reward prediction accuracy compared to a model focused solely on frame prediction. This improvement highlights the potential benefit of separating the prediction task for the reward signal.

The core contribution of this work is the novel architectural design that integrates reward prediction within the frame prediction framework. While the specific domain (Atari games) and the nature of the reward (often a simple scalar) might limit direct applicability, the approach demonstrates that modeling the reward explicitly can improve its prediction accuracy.

The synthetic data generation results are promising. Our model can generate plausible future trajectories, including both visual and reward information. This capability can be leveraged to augment training data for RL agents, potentially accelerating learning, especially in environments where real interaction is costly or time-consuming.

Future work could explore:
*   Different architectures for the reward decoder.
*   Incorporating the predicted rewards into the frame prediction process or vice versa.
*   Evaluating on more complex tasks with temporally correlated rewards.
*   Investigating the use of more sophisticated loss functions (e.g., incorporating uncertainty estimates).

**7. Conclusion**

We have presented a novel model for joint video and reward prediction in Atari games. By explicitly modeling the reward signal, our approach achieves significantly better reward prediction accuracy than existing frame-only models. The model's ability to generate synthetic trajectories with predicted rewards also shows promise for data-efficient reinforcement learning. This work contributes to the broader goal of building accurate predictive models for interactive environments.

The input consists of `n` consecutive frames, each of size `H x W x C`. To handle the sequential input, we process each frame in the sequence through a separate branch of the network (or use a 1D convolution across the time dimension if the input format allows) before fusing the representations. **5. Discussion**

This work demonstrates the feasibility of training a single model to predict both the next frame and the immediate reward in Atari games. By leveraging the data used for frame prediction, the model implicitly learns aspects of the game state that correlate with reward signals, enabling it to make reasonable predictions without explicit reward supervision during training.

The primary contribution lies in the joint prediction setup and the demonstration that accurate reward prediction is achievable in this context. This offers a promising avenue for improving model-based RL. In traditional model-based RL, the environment model typically includes state transition dynamics and reward functions. Training these components separately can be challenging and time-consuming. Our model provides a unified representation that could potentially be used to extract both the dynamics (via frame prediction) and the reward function (via reward prediction) more efficiently.

However, the current model has limitations. The reward prediction accuracy, while non-zero, might not be sufficient for complex RL tasks requiring precise reward signals. Furthermore, the model is trained solely on frame sequences and does not incorporate action information, which is crucial for predicting state transitions in many environments. Future work could explore incorporating action inputs or predicting longer-term rewards.

**6. Conclusion**

We have presented a novel approach for joint prediction of frames and rewards in Atari games. By extending a standard frame prediction model with a dedicated reward head, we demonstrate that accurate reward prediction is learnable from the same data used for frame prediction. This capability provides a richer environmental model that could significantly accelerate reinforcement learning, particularly model-based methods, by enabling earlier and potentially more informative feedback during the learning process. Future research will explore integrating this joint prediction model directly into RL frameworks.
