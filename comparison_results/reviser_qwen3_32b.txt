**1. Introduction**  
Model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) relies on accurate environment models to plan and learn policies efficiently. In deterministic environments like Atari games, reward signals are often tightly coupled with state transitions, raising the question: *Can reward prediction be inferred implicitly from state modeling, or does explicit joint modeling offer advantages for broader applicability?* While prior work (e.g., Oh et al., 2015) has demonstrated the feasibility of state prediction, this paper investigates the incremental value of explicitly modeling rewards alongside frames.  

We argue that even in deterministic settings, explicit joint modeling provides a structured framework for future work in stochastic environments, where reward-state dependencies are less predictable. By establishing a baseline for joint prediction in Atari, this work lays the groundwork for extending MBRL to more complex, real-world scenarios.  

---  

**2. Methodology**  
Our approach builds on prior work in visual state representation learning (Oh et al., 2015) and extends it with a reward prediction head. Below, we compare our methodology to related work and justify our design choices.  

**2.1 Comparison with Prior Work**  
Oh et al. (2015) focus on predicting game scores as a proxy for reward modeling. In contrast, our method explicitly predicts per-step rewards using a separate neural network head, trained alongside the frame prediction module. Key differences include:  
- **Loss Function**: We use a weighted combination of mean squared error (MSE) for frame prediction and binary cross-entropy for reward prediction.  
- **Reward Head Design**: Our reward head is a lightweight multi-layer perceptron (MLP), whereas Oh et al. rely on a single scalar output for cumulative score estimation.  

These modifications aim to disentangle reward modeling from long-term state prediction, enabling more flexible adaptation to stochastic environments.
