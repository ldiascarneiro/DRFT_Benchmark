# Incremental Nature of Reward Prediction in Atari Games

## Abstract

We present a method for predicting both frame sequences and rewards in Atari games, building upon previous work that focused solely on frame prediction. Our approach integrates a reward prediction head into an existing frame prediction model, leveraging the deterministic relationship between states and rewards in Atari environments. While our results are not surprising given this deterministic nature, they serve as a foundation for future experiments aimed at augmenting training with artificial samples and incorporating Monte-Carlo tree search.

## Introduction

In reinforcement learning (RL), the ability to learn both system dynamics and reward structures is crucial for model-based approaches. Traditionally, these components have been studied separately, with frame prediction models focusing on the former and reward prediction models on the latter. Our work aims to integrate these two areas, providing a more comprehensive model that can be applied to environments where both state transitions and rewards are critical.

## Related Work

**Frame Prediction**: Recent advancements in deep learning have led to significant improvements in frame prediction, particularly in complex environments like Atari games. Models such as those proposed by Dosovitskiy et al. (2015) and Gregor et al. (2015) have demonstrated the ability to generate realistic sequences of frames, paving the way for more sophisticated applications in RL.

**Reward Prediction**: Prior work on reward prediction has focused on various environments, including Atari games. Bellemare et al. (2013) introduced the Arcade Learning Environment, which has been instrumental in evaluating RL agents. More recent efforts, such as those by Bellemare et al. (2016), have explored the integration of count-based exploration with intrinsic motivation, highlighting the importance of understanding reward structures.

**Integration**: Integrating frame prediction and reward prediction is a natural progression in the field of model-based RL. Our work builds upon these advancements, providing a unified model that can be applied to environments where both state transitions and rewards are critical.

## Model

Our model extends an existing frame prediction architecture by adding a reward prediction head. The architecture consists of a recurrent neural network (RNN) that processes sequences of frames and outputs both predicted frames and rewards. During training, the model is presented with sequences of frames and corresponding rewards, allowing it to learn the deterministic relationship between states and rewards in Atari games.

## Experiments

We evaluate our model on a set of Atari games, focusing on the incremental improvements over previous frame prediction models. Our results demonstrate the ability to accurately predict both frames and rewards, highlighting the deterministic nature of these relationships in Atari environments. While these results are not surprising, they provide a solid foundation for future experiments aimed at augmenting training with artificial samples and incorporating Monte-Carlo tree search.

## Discussion

The incremental nature of our work is a testament to the deterministic relationship between states and rewards in Atari games. While this determinism simplifies the task of reward prediction, it also highlights the broader applicability of our approach to environments where such relationships are less straightforward. Future work will explore the potential of augmenting training with artificial samples and adding Monte-Carlo tree search to enhance the model's performance in more complex scenarios.

## Conclusion

In conclusion, our work presents a method for predicting both frame sequences and rewards in Atari games, building upon previous advancements in frame prediction. While our results are not surprising given the deterministic nature of these relationships, they serve as a foundation for future experiments aimed at augmenting training with artificial samples and incorporating Monte-Carlo tree search. This approach has the potential to significantly impact model-based RL, offering a more comprehensive understanding of both system dynamics and reward structures.
